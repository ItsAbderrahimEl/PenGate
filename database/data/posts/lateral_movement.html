Spreading Through the Network: Lateral Movement in Pentesting

Once inside a compromised system, skilled attackers rarely stop there. Lateral movement is the process of navigating through a network to access other systems, gather intelligence, and reach high-value targets. For penetration testers, mastering this phase is essential to simulate real-world threats and uncover hidden weaknesses in network architecture.

Key Takeaways:

  • Credential Reuse: Harvested credentials are often valid across multiple machines. Pentesters use tools like CrackMapExec or PsExec to authenticate across systems and move laterally.
  • Pass-the-Hash / Pass-the-Ticket: Instead of cracking passwords, attackers can use NTLM hashes or Kerberos tickets to authenticate without ever knowing the clear-text password.
  • Remote Services: Services like RDP, WinRM, SMB, and SSH are common pathways for lateral movement. Weak configurations or poor segmentation make these routes dangerously easy to exploit.
  • Active Directory Abuse: Enumerating domain trusts, user privileges, and Group Policy Objects (GPOs) allows attackers to leverage domain functionality to escalate and pivot further.

"Compromise one host, map the entire network." - Red Team Tactic

Lateral movement is about more than just hopping between machines — it’s about understanding trust relationships, privilege boundaries, and where valuable data resides. This phase is where attackers truly infiltrate and control an organization.

Defenders must deploy internal segmentation, strong logging, and behavioral monitoring to detect this type of movement. Preventing lateral movement means stopping attackers from turning a single foothold into total compromise.
