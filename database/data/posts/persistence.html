Staying Hidden: Persistence Techniques in Penetration Testing

In penetration testing and red teaming, persistence refers to the ability to maintain access to a compromised system even after reboots or detection attempts. Once access is gained, a smart attacker ensures they won’t need to break in again — this is where persistence techniques come into play.

Key Takeaways:

  • Startup & Scheduled Tasks: Creating scheduled tasks or modifying startup folders/registry keys ensures payloads or shells are re-executed upon system boot or user login.
  • Abusing Legitimate Tools: Using trusted tools like certutil, PowerShell, or WMI to maintain stealthy backdoors minimizes the risk of detection from AV or EDR.
  • DLL Hijacking & Services: By planting malicious DLLs or modifying Windows services, attackers can keep access even after a system reboot, especially on high-value servers or domain controllers.
  • Backdooring Accounts: Creating hidden users or adding SSH keys to authorized files allows persistent access without needing to exploit vulnerabilities again.

"Initial access gets you in. Persistence ensures you stay in." – Red Team Doctrine

Persistence is often subtle and difficult to detect. It's designed not to trigger alarms — which is why it’s so dangerous. Ethical hackers test these techniques to reveal how long an attacker could remain in a system undetected.

For defenders, identifying and neutralizing persistence mechanisms is a key component of incident response and digital forensics. Tools like Sysinternals, Autoruns, and EDR platforms can help uncover stealthy implants.
